Visualization

If we want to visualize diferent voting systems, than first we are going to need a way to visualize the voters' and the candidates' opinions. To put it simply: in our visualization if two people are close to each other, than their opinion is similar. If they are far away, than teir opinion is quite different. So we can place these lovely people into an opinion plane, where everyone's coordinate coresponds to their opinion.

In our visualization the voters are marked with a smaller circle, and the candidates are marked with a bigger circle.

Take the following picture for example:

There are 2 candidates and one voter in this picture. The orange voter is closer to the green candidate, than the pink candidate. This means that if the orange voter has to chose between these two candidates, it will chose green.

So when there is an election every voters' dream is that, the candidate closest to it gets elected. Our goal is to make sure, that the candidate, who is the closest to the average opinion gets elected.

Voter Types

Inside the simulation, the placement of a voter on the x and y axis represents their opinion. This stands for the candidates, too, meaning the closer the voter is to a candidate's position, the more similar their opinions are.

There are two types of voters, strategic and honest ones. You can find detailed information about them below.

  • Honest

    Honest voters vote for the candidate with whom their views align with the most. They do not consider the candidate's chances of winning, and they do not change their preference based on that. They are represented with a lighter color in the simulation.

  • Strategic

    Strategic voters, also called insincere or sophisticated voters may support another candidate more strongly than their sincere preference in order to prevent an undesirable outcome. They are represented with a darker outline in the simulation.

Voting Systems

The ideal electoral system is the one that straightforwardly and most accurately represents the preferences of voters. This is the goal every voting system strives for. You can find detailed information about the most used voting systems below. They are listed with increasing complexity.

  • Approval

    Approval voting systems are the first of multiple choice voting systems in our list. In this voting system basicly every voter can vote to as many candidates as they like. So for example if a voter would be satisfied if either ● candidate#1 or ● candidate#2 won the election, than the voter would vote for both ● candidate#1 and ● candidate#2. So it's almost like plurality, but here the voters' choices are not limited to one candidate.

    If we want to model this behavior, than we will need to define an approval range. If a candidate is inide of a voter's approval range, than the voter is going to vote for that candidate. If 2 candidates are inside the voter's approval range, than the voter is going to vote for those 2 canididates. In our idealized simulation every voter has the same approval range. So if we draw a circle around each candidate with the radius of the approval range, than every voter inside of that approval range is going to vote for that candidate.   • Borda counting

    Borda counting is ranked preferential votingmethod. Each voter ranks the candidates in a way, that the candidate who they the most want to win takes up the first place. And the candidate, who they the least want to win takes up the last place. Based on this tier-list every candidate gets points. For every first place in a tier-list the candidate gets as many points, as many candidates there are. For every second place in a tier-list, the candidate gets one less points, than the total number of candidates and so on. Each candidate gets one point from each last place in a tier-list. In the end the candidate with the most points wins.

  • Instant-runoff

    Instant-runoff voting(IRV) is a type of ranked preferential voting counting method used in single-winner elections with more than two candidates. IRV is also sometimes referred to as preferential voting. Like any ranked ballot voting systems, instead of indicating supprot for only one candidate, voters can rank the candidates in order of preference. Ballots are initially counted for each voter's top choice. If a candidate has more than 50% of the vote based on first-choices, that candidate wins. If not, the candidate with the fewest number of votes is eliminated, and the voters who chose that candidate as their first preference have their second preference votes considered, and added to the totals of the respective candidate's votes. This process is repeated until a candidate has more than half of the votes. If the field is reduced to two, an instant runoff is held, that allows the comparison of the top two candidates head-to-head.Instant-runoff voting is used in national elections in several countries, political parties, and private associations.
